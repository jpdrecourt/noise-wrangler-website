Wash away pesticides from your fruit and vegetables

An evidence-based protocol that removes 80-96% of pesticide residues using common kitchen ingredients

The Protocol

The 80/20 approach: daily quick wash (20-77% removal):

  1. Rinse: 30-60 seconds under running water while rubbing firmly
  2. Scrub: Use hands for soft produce, clean brush for firm items
  3. Focus: Pay attention to crevices and surface irregularities

For maximum effectiveness (80-96% removal):

  1. Prepare solution: Mix 1 tablespoon baking soda per liter of room temperature water
  2. Soak: Submerge produce for 12-15 minutes, gently agitating every 3-4 minutes
  3. Optional enhancement: Add 2 tablespoons white vinegar after the baking soda soak
  4. Rinse thoroughly: 30 seconds under running water
  5. Dry: Pat with clean towels or air dry

Special cases:

  • Fuzzy fruits (peaches, kiwi): Running water wash only - gentle handling prevents damage
  • Leafy greens with high neonicotinoid risk (cauliflower, lettuce, spinach): Brief blanch (30-60 seconds) before washing for systemic pesticides

The Science Behind It

Why baking soda works: Alkaline conditions (pH 8.5) chemically break down pesticides through hydrolysis while mechanical action dislodges surface residues. This method completely removes surface pesticides and degrades bound residues in fruit wax layers.

Why the sequential approach helps: Alkaline hydrolysis transforms pesticides into breakdown products that are 5-1000x more water-soluble. Adding vinegar afterward optimizes removal of these breakdown products through pH-dependent solubility changes. Industrial food processing validates this alkaline-then-acidic sequence as superior to single-stage treatments.

What doesn't work: Commercial produce washes, soap solutions, and salt water provide no advantage over plain water. The popular advice to remove stem cavities is also scientifically unsupported - pesticides concentrate in peels, not internal cavities.

The Neonicotinoid Problem

Certain crops carry higher risk from neonicotinoids - systemic pesticides that penetrate throughout plant tissues and cannot be removed by any washing method. Highest-risk crops include:

  • Leafy greens: Cauliflower (57.5% detection), lettuce (45.6%), spinach (38.7%)
  • Fruits: Cherries (45.9% detection), grapes (28.9%)
  • Field crops: Corn (95% of acres treated), soybeans (34-44% treated)

For these crops, brief heat treatment (30-60 seconds blanching) before washing can increase removal from 35% to 92% for surface-applied neonicotinoids.

Key Limitations

No washing method achieves 100% removal. Systemic pesticides from seed treatments remain in plant tissues regardless of washing technique. Even the most effective methods only remove surface residues and pesticides trapped in outer layers.

Bottom Line

Proper washing reduces pesticide exposure by 20-96% depending on method and pesticide type. The baking soda/vinegar protocol provides maximum effectiveness with minimal time investment, while understanding crop-specific risks helps prioritize when to use enhanced methods.
