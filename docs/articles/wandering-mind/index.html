An instrument for a fuzzy human

A fuzzy human

I understood early on that I wasn't cut out for classical guitar. The instrument demands millimetre and millisecond precision, earned through thousands of repetitions of the same piece. But I'm a little clumsy. Add to that my wandering mind's stubborn allergy to doing the same gesture twenty times over, and you get someone the classical method discourages fast.

I could have persisted, conformed to the instrument the way I was encouraged to. I made the opposite choice: to design an instrument that works with my imprecision instead of against it. There's a word for that quality of approximation: fuzzy. I hold on to it, because it names a starting condition. My imprecision isn't a flaw to correct. It's a characteristic to work with, the way a carpenter works with the grain of the wood.

The result is called the Chaos Conductor. It's an electronic musical instrument I play live, built from concrete sounds — noises from the world, recorded, then replayed and transformed on stage. I've been using it for two years now, solo and in a group.

Setting conditions, letting the machine decide

On an ordinary instrument, every gesture produces a sound directly. I pluck a string, the note sounds. I press a key, it resonates. This direct link between gesture and sound is what engineers call first-order control. It's wonderfully precise, and that precision is exactly what defeats me: it demands that my hand land in the right place at the right millisecond, thousands of times over.

The Chaos Conductor works differently. I never trigger a sound by hand. I set conditions. I fix, say, a density of events, a probability that a sound will occur, and it's the machine that decides the exact moment each sound appears. A potentiometer controls the rate at which sounds fire, from complete silence to a driving downpour. I turn that knob and I reshape a rhythm I can neither control in detail nor get wrong. I shape the behaviour of the sound; the machine decides the "when."

In technical terms, this is second-order control: I act on the conditions under which the system operates. That small distance between my gesture and the sound smooths out my approximations. Where a hesitant hand would produce a wrong note, here it produces only a slightly different density. The mistake, quite simply, no longer exists in the form that used to terrify me.

A conductor with no score

The spark came from a concert. I watched Rob Mazurek conduct the Exploding Star Orchestra, an ensemble from the Chicago scene steeped in free improvisation. Mazurek wasn't beating time to a written score. He was shaping, through gesture, music that emerged fresh from the collective playing. He set a frame and delegated the execution to the musicians.

That's exactly the relationship I have with my machines. It's where the instrument's name comes from. Where Mazurek addresses musicians, I address software that produces sounds. I set conditions, I delegate the execution, I shape the probability field from which sounds eventually emerge. A classical instrument speaks in the first person: I pluck, I blow. At the other extreme, certain autonomous music programs play entirely on their own, in the third person: they listen, decide, play, and the human becomes no more than a peer, an interlocutor, or a witness. The Chaos Conductor sits in the second person. I address the machine, I point it in a direction, and it answers in its own way.

Barreiro, its shellfish and its pontoon

All this stays abstract until you've heard what it's made of. Take a concrete project: Barreiro Improvised, selected for the OUT.RA creation grant in 2024. Barreiro is an industrial town across the Tagus from Lisbon. I built a series of performances from its sounds, recorded in the field and supplemented by a local sound archive. On stage, I was joined by Bruno Afonso on drums and Sara Zita Correia, on drums, voice and sound objects. The concert, recorded after a week's residency at the AMAC auditorium in Barreiro, is online.

Every sound has to be prepared to enter the instrument's logic, where it will be repeated at unpredictable intervals and layered with others. Knives scraping shellfish, recorded during a communal low-tide gathering, became short percussive elements, made to converse with the drums. The creaks and rattles of a metal pontoon at the ferry landing stayed as long events with a marked structure, holding the foreground. The crackling embers of a sardine barbecue were smoothed into a background wash.

The artistic gesture lives in these choices, and in the effects I apply. I play on the tension between raw sound and processed sound: a bitcrush brings out the granular texture of the embers, a delay makes the knife-scrapes more organic, a resonant bandpass filter accentuates the crushing character of the pontoon.

The best of it happens in playing together. For the opening of the second set, we discovered that the drummer could make a gong ring out. I resampled that resonance live, looped it, and it became the sonic backdrop for a good part of the set. He needed to know the instrument was capable of that, to really play with it.

"I have to be more attentive to the sounds, to what Jean-Philippe records while I'm playing and then plays back throughout the set. I've learned to listen to and respect the silences."
— Bruno Afonso

He hears his own drumming come back to him, transformed, redistributed, and responds to material that's partly his own.

The price to pay

This arrangement has a cost. By delegating the exact moment to the machine, I give up part of the gesture, that direct contact between hand and sound every instrumentalist knows. Some musicians live for the adrenaline of crossing a room on a tightrope; my instrument is built without that edge, because I didn't want it. It's a choice, and it has its trade-off.

The sharpest limitation lies elsewhere. Music is a time-based art, and one of its strengths is memory: a motif that returns, a phrase you recognise, anchor the listener and give a piece its shape. But every sound in the Chaos Conductor triggers in its own corner, with no link to the others. The instrument can say where it is in the sonic space; it struggles to say how it moves through it. It manipulates states, nouns of a sort — density, frequency, gain. It's missing the verbs: remember, develop, build a structure. Solo, my early videos now strike me as sounds juxtaposed without purpose. Playing in a group eases this shortfall, because the other musicians bring the structure the instrument lacks. That's the work in progress, the one I'm still tackling.

Finding the stage again

The essential thing, for me, fits in one sentence: I got back on stage. Stepping back from controlling every note to setting conditions removed the anxiety I associated with it. Hearing the soundscape evolve under my fingers and adjusting densities feels more musical to me than playing guitar, because I'm finally working with my approximation instead of fighting it. My wandering mind finds its match here: curious about a region of the sonic space, I can explore it on the spot, without having to rehearse my way there, and move elsewhere the moment I feel like it. I built an instrument that reflects my mind and my gesture, and that, by extension, invites my audience and the people who play with me to listen differently.
