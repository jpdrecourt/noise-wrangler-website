The AI Archetype Council

Multiple AI agents, each rooted in a different intellectual tradition, challenge your thinking in parallel. The voices evolve with every session.

Get it

  1. Download from github.com/jpdrecourt/AI-Archetype-Council
  2. Copy the commands/ and skills/ folders into your project's .claude/ directory (or ~/.claude/ for global access)
  3. Run /council Your topic here

MIT licensed. Built for Claude Code, adaptable to any multi-agent AI setup.

What it does

You give the council a topic. A Convener selects 3-4 voices from a pool of ten archetypes. Each voice runs as an independent AI agent with its own worldview. They respond in parallel, turn by turn. You react, they respond again. The same question looks different to The Engineer (systems, constraints, feedback loops) and The Hermit (what if you did less?).

The voices don't share context within a turn. This independence is what produces actual diversity of perspective.

The voices

  • Engineer: systems thinking, cybernetics, constraints
  • Alchemist: transformation stages, what must break down first
  • Craftsperson: embodied practice, prototyping, material resistance
  • Critic: art-historical context, lineage, who came before
  • Child: pre-categorical perception, "but why?"
  • Griot: oral tradition, cycles, the story underneath
  • Hermit: subtraction, silence, what's already sufficient
  • Phenomenologist: lived experience before theory
  • Spirit Walker: non-human agencies, what the material wants

Voices that evolve

AI models are stateless. Every conversation starts from zero. The council's voice definitions live in plain text files that persist between sessions. After each conversation, the system reviews what happened and updates the voice files: blind spots get corrected, framings get sharpened, lessons from the user get recorded.

This feedback loop builds something like personality over time. After 20 sessions, The Craftsperson knows when a limitation lives in the design, not in the hands. The Convener knows that philosophical insights work better reframed as design methodology. Your council diverges from mine, shaped by different questions and different corrections.

Make it yours

These voices serve my practice as a sound artist and generative systems designer. Swap them. Give them names. Make them domain-specific. Add new voices by dropping a markdown file in the voices/ folder. The format is simple: a tradition, an attention pattern, a distrust, a core question, and a couple of paragraphs on how the voice thinks.

Cost

Each voice runs on Claude Sonnet with a compact prompt (around 800 words) and returns 2-3 sentences per turn. The independence constraint keeps costs down: each agent has a small context window. Only the Convener accumulates the full session history. A typical session costs less than a single long conversation with a frontier model.

Update — March 7, 2026

After a few sessions I reworked the voice mechanics. The original version re-launches each voice from scratch every turn — simple and cheap. The new version makes voices persistent: launched once, then resumed across turns via their agent ID. Each voice remembers its own reasoning chain, not just a summary of what came before. This makes multi-turn sessions noticeably sharper — voices build on their own thinking and engage with each other's specific claims rather than reacting to summaries.

The new version also adds reaction rounds (an optional intra-turn exchange when genuine disagreement surfaces) and explicit instructions for cross-voice engagement — challenging, building on, or naming convergence between positions. It costs more tokens over long sessions, but the deliberation quality is worth it.

Both versions are now in the repo: /council (persistent) and /council-lite (stateless). The README has a comparison table.
